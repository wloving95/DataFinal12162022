Poverty and Public Health in America

By Weston Loving

Poverty and obesity are two words that over the last few decades have become intertwined with one another, as well as with American culture. Citizens from other countries look at America and images of McDonaldâ€™s, tent cities, and a lack of social safety nets for our population. While a true American conservative would say that it is up to each individual to ensure their own success, putting the public health and public wealth crises into a geographical visual helps to show just how disadvantaged people from certain regions of the United States really are.

While the first map shows the average percentage of people who live below the poverty level in each state over the last three years, it does not show the effects of poverty. The effects of poverty are evident when a person goes shopping, what types of consumables they buy, or do not buy. When there is a limited amount of money, one can does not splurge on vegetables, at least if they believe that the vegetables are more expensive than pre-packaged frozen dinners, because time is of the essence as well. When available options are reduced due to price, the healthiest options are not the ones being chosen. As is shown in the second map, the differences in obesity levels across state lines are eerily like the differences in poverty levels across state lines. Speaking to one shopper at a Stop & Shop supermarket, who wished to remain anonymous, she said, "Everything is going up in price, I used to be able to feed my family for about half of the price of what my weekly groceries cost now." That is the dilemma that every American family is facing as inflation worsens.

In general, states with a high level of poverty tend to have a higher rate of obesity among their residents. Poverty and Obesity rates are much higher in the Midwest and the South than they are in the rest of the country. While people can point to any number of solutions, it seems that reducing the number of people in poverty will result in a healthier nation.
